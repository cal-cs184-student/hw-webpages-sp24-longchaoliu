CS184 Cloth Simulation

Rohan Mathur and Joy Liu | Spring 2024

Overview

In the project, we learned how to simulate the physical properties of cloth by modeling it as a grid of springs. The springs determine the rigidity of cloth. We used integration to determine each grid point's position as a function of external forces like gravity and internal forces from the other springs. Next, we enforced that the cloth could not pass through other primitives in the scene, nor could it collide with itself.  Finally, we gave the cloth different textures by mapping the points according to normal and height scaling functions and coloring them according to basic ray tracing and texture mapping.

Part 2. Simulation via numerical integration

  • Experiment with some the parameters in the simulation. To do so, pause the simulation at the start with P, modify the values of interest, and then resume by pressing P again. You can also restart the simulation at any time from the cloth's starting position by pressing R.
  • Describe the effects of changing the spring constant ks; how does the cloth behave from start to rest with a very low ks? A high ks?
  • With lower spring constant, the fabric drapes much lower. It doesn’t retain tension as well, and so feels the effects of gravity much more. With higher spring constant, it rests higher in elevation, closer to the pinned points. You can imagine pulling a spring more taut, longer than its resting length, in which case it’ll be totally tight and straight.
  • 50 N/m
  • 10000 N/m
  • 40000 N/m
  • What about for density?
  • Increasing the density has a similar effect to increasing the spring constant–a heavier cloth will be pulled more by the force of gravity.
  • 1000 g/cm^2
  • 10 g/cm^2
  • What about for damping?
  • With no damping, energy is never lost in the system, so the cloth will continue bouncing for the rest of time. With any amount of damping, it will eventually reach a resting point. The higher the damping factor, the less time it oscillates for. For example, with damping 100%, it will not bounce at all, instantly achieving its resting state. Some damping has the effect of simulating a heavier cloth, but 100% looks quite unnatural.

Part 3. Handling collisions with other objects

  • Show us screenshots of your shaded cloth from scene/sphere.json in its final resting state on the sphere using the default ks = 5000 as well as with ks = 500 and ks = 50000. Describe the differences in the results.
  • As before, the higher the spring constant, the more rigid the cloth is, because the greater the tension is. In the first case, the cloth drapes very naturally, hugging the contours of the upper hemisphere. There are 8 visible creases. In the second case, there are less creases in the cloth (around 6 visible), because it’s harder to “squeeze” the springs, and the cloth appears more untucked, but the four corners of the cloth are still pointing downward. In the final case, there are about 4 visible creases, and the cloth appears extremely rigid, so much so that the corners are no longer pointing at the ground, meaning the spring force relative to gravity is now much more significant than before.

Part 5: Shaders

  • Explain in your own words what is a shader program and how vertex and fragment shaders work together to create lighting and material effects.
  • A shader program takes in lighting and input geometry and returns color and a new geometry in order to generalize textures. The vertex function is called first, on the input geometry and alters it. The fragment shader takes in the new geometry and returns color values.
  • Explain the Blinn-Phong shading model in your own words. Show a screenshot of your Blinn-Phong shader outputting only the ambient component, a screen shot only outputting the diffuse component, a screen shot only outputting the specular component, and one using the entire Blinn-Phong model.
  • The Blinn-Phong model combines three different kinds of light: ambient light which comes from all directions, and so is always included at the same brightness at every point; diffuse light, which produces a matte effect from light bouncing in many directions; and specular lighting, a mirror-like reflection from a single direction.
